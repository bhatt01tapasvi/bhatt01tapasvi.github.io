Backend engineering on Twitch's video networking team

I spent two years wrestling with the complexities of the Twitch video system and doing my best to contribute useful services where I can. Below are some brief summaries of the projects I worked on. Several details are omitted due to non-disclosure agreement.

Porting distributed locking system to AWS DynamoDB

  • Project: Amazon IVS
  • Timeline: March 2022 - August 2023

In order to increase the redundancy of a service (details omitted), I had to port its distributed locking system from a third party service to AWS DynamoDB so that multiple entities could view the lock state of other entities around them. This required a deep understanding of how the system's locking actually worked, and writing rigorous tests to ensure that the overhaul was actually correct. To use a good phrase from a mentor of mine, I felt that this project bizarrely validated most traditional computer science undergraduate curricula.

Minimal-effort packet disappearance detection

  • Project: Amazon IVS
  • Timeline: August 2021 - August 2022

My first project as a junior engineer was to build a service that detects abrupt drops in certain arenas of the video system's traffic. These drops are colloquially referred to as 'black holes', and the service is somewhat cheekily called LIGO. LIGO runs on AWS ECS/Fargate and connects to SQS, Kinesis, and Apache Druid. I architected, coded, and productionized the system entirely on my own, and gained a lot of experience on the journey.

Implementing unequal cost multipathing

  • Project: Amazon IVS
  • Timeline: January 2022 - April 2022
  • Mentors: Rob Mitchelmore

Unequal cost multipathing, or UCMP, allows more efficient allocation of network traffic across the video system. I updated the previous algorithm (which implemented equal cost multipathing, UCMP's more inefficent predecessor) and worked with a colleague on the networking team to test the code live on the network. This project was yet another step in my journey to understand how exactly the internet works, something that I likely will never understand.

Data pipelining service for real-time estimated viewership on third party CDNs

  • Project: Amazon IVS
  • Timeline: June 2020 - August 2020
  • Mentors: Kyle Lee, Jos Kraaijeveld

As an intern in the summer of 2020, I was tasked with building a service to track the Twitch video system's usage of third party CDNs which output a graph showing how many users were routed to CDNs per minute. I got my first taste of assembling AWS services into a single working program, and utilized Kinesis, Lambda, and CloudWatch.

Designing and writing curriculum for a GANs textbook chapter

Kids growing up in the age of AI need accessible online platforms with which to learn advanced concepts that will broaden their understanding of the computerized ecosystem they live in. The goal of GenAI was to design such a platform for learners of all levels. We intended to create a prototype to hand off to professional designers.

  • Project: GenAI Initiative
  • Timeline: October 2019 - June 2020
  • Team: Katherine Gallagher

The platform GenAI was intended to be an online textbook with several chapters about different AI topics, such as neural networks, conversational agents, and robotics, among several others. The chapter we decided to prototype with was the one about generative adversarial networks (GANs) due to the project lead's personal interest in them.

A great deal of time was spent on the interaction design of the website itself, and keeping both the landing pages and the learning material itself as easy to navigate as possible for learners and users of all ages and levels. For example, we determined that while the color scheme and design should remain the same throughout the website, the "feel" (font, UI) should change between content levels.

We also wanted to make AI seem approachable, not futuristic, dark, chrome colored, or hacker-y. We considered naturalistic themes and light colors in opposition to the typical depiction of AI in media.

At the beginning of the project, I worked along with another student in charge of web design to wireframe out the look and feel of the website. I went through several of the educational websites that I used on a daily basis and tried to pick out the elements that I found the most natural and usable, and then combine these into a design. The web designer took my fairly simple ideas and gave them a great deal of color.





Halfway through the project, however, the web designer got too busy, and I was left with coming up with the visual design elements of the website as well. In accordance with our naturalistic ideas for representing AI, I chose a color palette, designed a v1 logo, and created a moodboard.

I was also assigned to write the beginner and intermediate levels of a GANs textbook chapter. This was the first time that I had to think deeply about what it meant to be a learner at each of these levels, and be mindful to use appropriate terminology and complexity. Similarly, we designed the visuals to "level up" as well.



This project taught me a great deal about designing UI and thinking very deeply about my user. Though I had never dabbled in visual design before, I enjoyed the chance to determine how the site looked and felt, skills that proved extremely useful to designing this website!

Redesigning Santa Monica Studio's engine debugging system

When creating a game as huge as God of War, designers do an almost unimaginable amount of tweaking and twiddling with different game engine properties to get the gameplay feeling just right. My task as an intern was to create a prototype for an updated engine debugging menu. Several details are omitted due to non-disclosure agreement.

  • Project: God of War: Ragnarok
  • Timeline: May 2019 - August 2019
  • Mentor: Alden Chew

Like many triple A game studios, Santa Monica Studio utilizes a custom built engine for God of War. As such, the tool for tweaking settings within said engine was also custom designed, and was over a decade old when I was tasked with creating a more modern prototype.

I was tasked with using the Dear ImGui library to create this prototype. ImGui is an open source C++ library for creating user interfaces, primarily for game engines and other real-time 3D applications.



This was my first time working with a codebase on a massive scale. I learned how to parse through huge chunks of relatively undocumented code, and gained an intuition for how to look for the code actually relevant to my needs. I had also never worked with an external library as big as ImGui, so figuring out how to integrate it into the existing PlayStation codebase was a slow task.

I also learned what it was like to have multiple stakeholders for a system. Designers, artists, and programmers all used the engine debugging tool in different ways, and had different opinions on the changes that should be made to it. From the way the PS4 controller was used to navigate the menu, to the actual widgets used for certain features themselves, I learned the importance of taking multiple voices into account when creating an end product for a variety of users.

The biggest challenge I faced was struggling through the nuances of C++. I learned that low-level programming is definitely not my forte, and the intricacies of buffer overflows and overwritten caches did not bring me much joy. But in the end, the experience of working in a triple A studio on such a huge project was worth all the frustration. As a small contributor, I'm excited for the world to see God of War: Ragnarok, and as a fan of the franchise, I'm dying to play it myself!

EDC: Developing data science curriculum for high school sciences

I was a design collaborator for the Science+C initiative through the Educational Development Center. I designed a three day data science curriculum for integration into high school biology, chemistry, and physics classes. I ran workshops with educators and iterated using their feedback. The curriculum will be used by the State of Massachusetts.

  • Project: Science+C
  • Timeline: December 2020 - February 2021
  • Team: Irene Lee (PI), Claire Bao, Grace Kim, Erica Zhou

The Science+C project is a collaboration between the Massachusetts Department of Elementary and Secondary Education to create an alternate pathway to college and science careers for high school students by developing and testing three new computational science courses. These courses—Computational Biology, Computational Chemistry, and Computational Physics—will be available to all Massachusetts high schools beginning in the 2022-2023 school year.


I specifically worked as a design collaborator on the Data Science + Machine Learning units. The units were divided into data science, decision trees, clustering algorithms, and neural networks. I designed the data science curriculum, a 3 day workshop with a lesson and associated activities for each day.



I created lesson plans designed to introduce beginners to data science at a low level. I cover the basics of data science and what data scientists to, and designed activities to teach summary statistics and visualization usage in an interactive manner.



Activities included:
  • An unplugged activity in which students provide their own data and organize themselves into a dataset to learn summary statistics.
  • An adapted memory game for matching certain situations to the data visualization that would be the most appropriate.
  • Multiple R programming activities written in Google CoLab that walk students through analyzing datasets relevant to their high school science class.

I ran this curriculum in a workshop with several high school science educators and iterated on the activities with them. Their feedback was invaluable for iterating and improving on the activities.






The curriculum will continue to go through iteration cycles until it pilots with schools across the state of Massachusetts in September 2022.

Smart 3-in-1 Physical Separation Device for Plastic Recycling

So, July 2025. I found myself at the University of Tokyo’s International Research Camp, right next to the scenic Yamanaka River with Mount Fuji casually photobombing every group selfie. The camp gathered 46 participants - two seasoned researchers from the industry and a caffeine-charged squad of 44 PhD students hailing from ten universities across several countries and regions. Our mission? In teams of eight, dream up and design an innovative device for future factories.

  • Event: University of Tokyo International Summer Camp, July 2025
  • Project: Smart 3-in-1 Physical Separation Device
  • Team: Nadia Oktiarsy (UTokyo), Takumi Endo (UTokyo), Yours Truly ☺ (NTU), Ting-Wei Emily Chang (Rice University), Zheng Zhou (ZJU)

So what’s this “Smart 3-in-1 Physical Separation Device”? Glad you asked. It’s a high-tech plastic sorting wizard that combines three physical separation methods—electrostatic, acoustic resonance, and electromagnetic (EM) fields—to pull apart mixed plastics with over 90-95% accuracy. While commercial systems often rely on Near-Infrared (NIR) or visible light spectra, they stumble on dark or dirty plastics. Our device? It laughs in the face of moisture, darkness, and contamination.

Here’s how it works, step-by-step on the conveyor belt of fame:

  • Electrostatic Separation: Plastics get triboelectrically charged as they slide along the belt. Then, they pass through a high-voltage horizontal electric field (~40 kV), where their charge differences cause lateral deflection. This stage alone hits >95% sorting accuracy for many plastics.
  • Acoustic Resonance Separation: Plastics vibrate differently based on their stiffness (Young’s modulus). We pump ~40 Hz acoustic waves, and their resonance amplitudes vary. Acoustic modules use these subtle shifts to split plastics, achieving around ~90% accuracy.
  • Electromagnetic (EM) Separation: The real magic spark. A pulsed EM field at frequencies around 60 kHz and 100 kHz targets differential dielectric polarization dynamics. PET and PVC, for example, resonate with these frequencies differently—PVC, with chlorine atoms in its polymer chain, has higher dielectric loss and sticks to the belt longer at 100 kHz, while PET slides off. This stick-slip control lets us separate these plastics with >95% accuracy.

Why does this matter? Global plastics use is exploding—expected to nearly triple by 2060—and recycling rates still lag behind other materials. Mixed or contaminated waste often gets rejected or downcycled, losing valuable recyclable content. Our device aims to fix that by boosting sorting accuracy, reducing contamination, and enabling smarter factories without sacrificing throughput. Plus, it works well in real-world messy conditions—rain or shine, sticky or not.

From my side, I dove deep into the physics and system modelling for the electromagnetic module, tuning the pulsed field frequencies and stick-slip parameters to cleanly separate PET and PVC. While the team built the device, I pinged external APAC collaborators to help validate the models and coordinated testing plans. It’s no small feat handling dielectric polarization with ionic dynamics on a conveyor belt!

Our prototype processes 50–1,500 kg/hour depending on configuration, with a modest footprint that can scale up for industrial recycling lines. Budget-wise, setup costs range from US$118k to 195k, including maintenance. Not bad for a device that could significantly cut landfill waste, improve recycled polymer purity, and ramp up factory efficiency.

After weeks of whiteboards packed with equations, sushi breaks, and late-night rapid prototyping, we had a working system that could sort plastics reliably, fast, and with minimal fuss—even sometimes shocking the professors in the best way.

The best part? This isn’t just lab curiosity. It’s a stepping stone for circular economies, a boost to sustainable manufacturing, and a meaningful contribution to tackling one of the biggest waste challenges globally. Plus, I now inspect every plastic bottle like a hawk. No escape.

What's bettter? We won award for Best Innovation.

Heat Transfer, Vibration and Magnetic Effects in Rectangular Channel

When I began my research in thermal fluid dynamics, I was drawn to the complex interplay between heat transfer mechanisms and electromagnetic effects in confined geometries. The rectangular channel configuration presents unique challenges in understanding how magnetic fields influence convective heat transfer while simultaneously dealing with flow-induced vibrations. This interdisciplinary approach combining thermal sciences, fluid mechanics, and electromagnetic theory became the foundation of my most technically challenging research project.

Experimental Investigation of Magnetohydrodynamic Heat Transfer with Vibrational Effects

  • Timeline: January 2024 - June 2024
  • Mentors: Dr. Sarah Mitchell

In my first comprehensive experimental study, I investigated the coupled effects of magnetic field orientation, channel vibration frequency, and heat transfer enhancement in rectangular channels. Through systematic parametric analysis, we identified critical thresholds for magnetic field strength where heat transfer coefficients showed significant enhancement due to secondary flow generation and boundary layer disruption.

I also developed a computational framework based on magnetohydrodynamic principles to predict optimal operating conditions for maximum heat transfer efficiency. This led to some remarkable correlations between Hartmann number and Nusselt number variations, shown below. This work is under submission to the Journal of Heat and Mass Transfer - awaiting peer review.



PIV Analysis of Flocking Behavior: Vicsek Models and Cross-Correlation Algorithms

  • Timeline: April 2024 - June 2024
  • Collaborators: Dr. Michael Chen, Dr. Elena Rodriguez, James Park, Sarah Kim, Alex Thompson, Maria Garcia, David Lee, Lisa Wang, Tom Anderson

As a computational physicist specializing in collective behavior, I applied Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) techniques to analyze emergent flocking patterns in biological systems. Using advanced cross-correlation algorithms and Vicsek model implementations, we developed novel approaches to quantify collective motion dynamics. The research focused on understanding how local interaction rules lead to global coherent movement patterns observed in bird flocks, fish schools, and bacterial colonies.

  • Collaborators: Dr. Jennifer Walsh, Prof. Robert Martinez, Dr. Anna Kowalski, Emma Chen, Marcus Johnson

I'm currently working with an international consortium to develop comprehensive educational frameworks for misinformation literacy. Our approach combines cognitive science principles with practical digital literacy skills to help students and educators worldwide identify, analyze, and counter false information. The program includes interactive modules, real-world case studies, and assessment tools designed for diverse cultural and educational contexts.



How games support mental health and eudaimonic well-being

I'm only in my mid 20s, but people my age have lived through a huge amount of unrest - the first Trump administration, COVID, and the second - much more fascist - Trump administration, with very little mental break in between. In both personal and environmental times of darkness, I find myself going back to the games that have taught me strength, given me characters to model myself after, and given me a place where I feel like I can fight back against evil. I firmly believe that playing games like Final Fantasy VII in my formative years - all about how a giant corporation destroys the world through capitalism and greed - has been key to shaping my own will to live on and fight on. With that in mind, the area I hope to write my dissertation on someday is in how playing games can support eudaimonic well-being, human flourishing, and mental health. Games for mental health are often discussed in the language of escapism, but I think there are a lot of important lessons we can learn from our ability to agentically interact with stories of resistance, resilience, and heroism within games.

Investigating the effects of eudaimonic gaming experiences

  • Timeline: October 2024 - present
  • Mentors: Julie Kientz, Jin Ha Lee

After wading around in readings about self-determination theory, narrative games, and eudaimonic well-being, I identified a theoretical gap that had mysteriously gone unfilled. Media studies scholars have elaborated extensively on why a person may seek out a eudaimonic gaming experience, but the long lasting effects of such experiences were unknown. Partly motivated by getting to read about peoples' positive and life-changing experiences with games, I conducted a large survey that received 100+ responses, and conducted a structured deductive analysis to identify pathways to certain types of changes that people believed these gaming experiences had on their broader lives. As this work is under submission, I won't go into too much more detail, but I expect this to be the foundation of much of my future work.

Systematic review on narrative games for mental health

  • Timeline: June 2024 - present
  • Mentors: Julie Kientz

The games that have supported my mental health have always been narrative heavy RPGs. In my first year, I took a particular interest in narrative games, and like a good junior PhD student, I decided to do my first systematic review on how narrative game-based interveions are used in mental health research. Myself and Jamie Espinosa-Briones (DUB REU '24) reduced almost 2000 papers down to 17 games for analysis. A preliminary analysis (results to be presented at DiGRA '25) revealed that a majority of the games surrounded treatment for depression and anxiety; however, how these constructs were measured differed significantly between studies. In addition, theoretical underpinnings such as narrative theory (popuarlized by communications scholars) were often overlooked in the design of these games. In future discussion (journal paper coming soon), we intend to suggest design guidelines for further cross-collaboration between communication studies, mental health, and human-computer interaction.
